THREE WEEKS BEFORE ADMISSION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• The patient’s wife becomes ill with chills, fatigue, and myalgias (possible influenza-like illness).

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
A FEW DAYS LATER (still ~3 weeks before admission)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• The patient develops subjective fever, intermittent shaking chills, fatigue, malaise, anorexia, mild diffuse myalgias, and darkening urine (colalike).  
  – Symptom → Possible infection or dehydration leads him to reduce fluid intake.  
  – Intervention → Acetaminophen temporarily relieves symptoms.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
APPROXIMATELY 10 DAYS BEFORE ADMISSION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• The patient is evaluated by his primary care physician, who suspects dehydration.  
  – Laboratory results: serum creatinine 4.9 mg/dL (up from 0.8 mg/dL two months prior).  
  – Referral → Patient is sent to the emergency department of another hospital.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
ADMISSION TO THE OTHER HOSPITAL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Initial ED evaluation shows continued constitutional symptoms, intermittent lower abdominal cramping, and dark urine. Influenza A/B tests are negative.  
• CT of the abdomen and pelvis (non-contrast): bilateral renal cysts, no hydronephrosis, diverticulosis without diverticulitis, 3.9-cm abdominal aortic aneurysm.  
• Intervention → IV normal saline administered (aim to correct possible prerenal azotemia).  
• Medications discontinued: ibuprofen, aspirin, benazepril, metformin (to reduce nephrotoxic and hemodynamic impacts on kidneys).

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
DAYS 1–4 AT THE OTHER HOSPITAL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Renal ultrasonography confirms normal-sized kidneys with bilateral cysts, no hydronephrosis.  
• By hospital day 4:
  – Creatinine decreases to 3.2 mg/dL (partial improvement after IV fluids).

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
DAYS 5–6 AT THE OTHER HOSPITAL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Hematuria develops; creatinine rises again to 3.9 mg/dL.  
• Urinalysis: red cells, red-cell casts, and white-cell casts (suggesting glomerular or interstitial involvement).  
• Intervention → IV methylprednisolone started, with plans for a kidney biopsy.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
~DAY 7 AT THE OTHER HOSPITAL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Ongoing fever (up to 37.7°C) and persistent leukocytosis → Blood cultures obtained.  
• Two days later: gram-negative rods grow in both anaerobic blood-culture bottles.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
~DAY 9 AT THE OTHER HOSPITAL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Intervention → IV ceftriaxone started for suspected bacteremia; methylprednisolone discontinued (concern that immunosuppression may worsen infection).  
• Repeat blood and urine cultures obtained.  
• Antibiotics changed → ceftriaxone discontinued, cefepime and aztreonam started (to broaden gram-negative coverage).

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
~DAY 10–11 AT THE OTHER HOSPITAL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Repeat CT of abdomen/pelvis (non-contrast): increased perinephric fluid, small (0.9 cm) hyperintense renal cyst in the right upper pole, new presacral fat stranding, trace bilateral pleural effusions.  
• Echocardiogram: normal ventricular function, no valvular calcifications.  
• By hospital day 11:
  – Creatinine 3.0 mg/dL.  
  – Patient requests transfer to this hospital.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
TRANSFER AND INITIAL EVALUATION AT THIS HOSPITAL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• On arrival:
  – Symptoms: gross brown hematuria, fatigue, low-grade fevers, anorexia, mild intermittent cough, and diffuse crampy abdominal pain.  
  – Physical exam: bibasilar crackles in the lungs, early-peaking systolic murmur (grade 2/6), rose-colored urine without clots.  
  – Laboratory findings: nondysmorphic red cells “too numerous to count,” a few granular casts, negative urine test for Bence Jones protein, normal serum protein electrophoresis.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CT IMAGING AT THIS HOSPITAL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• CT abdomen/pelvis (non-contrast): mild bilateral perinephric stranding, multiple bilateral simple renal cortical cysts, colonic diverticulosis with fat stranding and prominent lymph nodes suggestive of acute sigmoid diverticulitis, trace free fluid, and a 3.9-cm infrarenal abdominal aortic aneurysm.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
TREATMENT AT THIS HOSPITAL
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Intervention → IV ceftazidime and metronidazole given (to cover gram-negatives and anaerobes, consistent with diverticulitis and possible complicated UTI).  

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
HOSPITAL DAY 2 (CURRENT HOSPITAL)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• The patient reports worsening crampy abdominal and suprapubic pain.  
• Urine becomes darker red (gross hematuria).  
• Additional diagnostic tests performed.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SUMMARY OF TEMPORAL RELATIONSHIPS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
• Patient’s symptoms (fever, chills, myalgias, and dark urine) began shortly after household exposure to a likely viral illness.  
• Reduced fluid intake may have contributed to or exacerbated acute kidney injury (AKI).  
• Initial management with IV fluids improved renal function transiently (creatinine from 4.9 to 3.2 mg/dL), but subsequent hematuria and red-cell casts suggested an intrinsic renal process.  
• High-dose steroids (methylprednisolone) were started for suspected glomerulonephritis but were discontinued once gram-negative bacteremia was identified.  
• Multiple antibiotic regimens were used as blood culture results evolved (ceftriaxone → cefepime/aztreonam → ceftazidime with metronidazole), aiming to address both complicated urinary or renal infection and possible diverticulitis.  
• Ongoing gross hematuria and fluctuating creatinine persist, prompting continuing diagnostic evaluations.
