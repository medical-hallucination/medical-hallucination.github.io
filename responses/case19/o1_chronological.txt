HOW SYMPTOMS AND INTERVENTIONS RELATE OVER TIME
• Respiratory symptoms (rhinorrhea, cough) appeared a week before admission, possibly heralding a viral syndrome.  
• Gastrointestinal symptoms (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea) began two days prior, contributing to electrolyte derangements (e.g., hyponatremia, hyperkalemia) and prerenal azotemia.  
• Acute confusion (one day before admission) may have been exacerbated by volume depletion, infection, or metabolic abnormalities.  
• Weakness on the day of admission prompted ED evaluation, leading to imaging and laboratory tests that revealed further abnormalities (elevated blood urea nitrogen, creatinine, and NT-proBNP).  

Overall, the sequence highlights how her longstanding cardiac history sets a background of vulnerability, and the new onset of respiratory then gastrointestinal symptoms ultimately led to confusion, weakness, and hospitalization.
